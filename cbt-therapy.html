CBT Therapy

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) is a structured, evidence-based talking therapy that explores the relationship between our thoughts, emotions, behaviours, and physical experiences.

It is based on the understanding that these aspects of human experience are closely interconnected and continually influence one another. By developing awareness of these connections, CBT helps people understand the patterns that shape how they experience themselves, others, and the world around them.

These patterns are viewed as understandable responses shaped by experiences, relationships, beliefs, and the meanings we have made over time. At times, these ways of interpreting ourselves and our circumstances can become limiting, contributing to anxiety, low mood, avoidance or self-criticism.

While CBT is often recognised for its practical and structured approach, it is also grounded in curiosity and self-understanding, by developing a deeper awareness of oneself, including personal values, experiences and strengths. This understanding can create opportunities for change and support a more compassionate relationship with oneself.

Ultimately, CBT is about more than reducing distress. It is about helping people develop insight into their experiences, reconnect with what matters to them, and build greater flexibility, resilience, and confidence in living their lives.

A substantial body of research supports the effectiveness of CBT across a wide range of mental health difficulties. Clinical guidelines from organisations such as the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) recommend CBT as a first-line treatment for many common mental health problems, including depression, generalised anxiety disorder, panic disorder, obsessive-compulsive disorder, post-traumatic stress disorder and social anxiety disorder.

Meta-analyses and systematic reviews have consistently demonstrated that CBT can significantly reduce symptoms, improve daily functioning, and help prevent relapse, particularly when individuals continue to use the skills they have developed after therapy has ended.

Because of its strong empirical foundation and focus on developing lifelong coping skills, CBT remains one of the most widely researched and widely used psychological therapies worldwide

Meet the therapist

Areas of Clinical Focus

01

Anxiety

Anxiety can leave you feeling trapped by worry, uncertainty, or fear. Together, we'll explore not only the patterns that maintain anxiety but also how it may be shaping the way you live your life. Therapy aims to help you move towards greater confidence, freedom, and engagement with what matters most to you.

02

Depression & Low Mood

Depression can affect how you see yourself, others, and the future, leaving life feeling empty, disconnected or meaningless. Together, we'll work towards understanding these patterns, and reconnecting with your values, strengths, and sense of purpose.

03

Trauma & PTSD

Traumatic experiences can change the way we experience ourselves, other people, and the world around us. Therapy provides a safe space to process these experiences, make sense of their impact, and support you in reclaiming and rebuilding your life, while moving forward at your own pace.

Therapy Format

Sessions

Online therapy via secure video conferencing platforms, allowing sessions to take place from a comfortable and convenient setting.

Frequency

Usually weekly or fortnightly sessions, depending on your individual needs and therapeutic goals.

Age group

Adults aged 18 and over.

Languages

Therapy is available in English and Greek.

Length of Therapy

CBT can be short-term and longer-term. During our initial assessment, we can think together about what may be most appropriate for you.

Getting Started

Taking the first step towards therapy can feel like a big decision. To help you decide whether CBT and my approach is right for you, I offer a free 15-minute initial conversation. This is an opportunity to ask any questions you may have, discuss what has brought you to therapy, and learn more about how I work.

If we decide that working together feels like a good fit, the next step is a 90-minute initial assessment. This provides dedicated time to explore your difficulties in more depth, understand your experiences, and identify your goals for therapy. If we decide to continue, we will agree on the length of therapy and discuss the therapeutic agreement.
